Executive Function

Executive function refers to mental skills that help people plan, organize, focus attention, control impulses, solve problems, and manage time effectively.

Strong executive function is linked to success in many areas of life, while weak executive function is associated with ADHD, autism and other conditions.

Key Components

  • Self-Control: Resisting distractions & impulses, delaying gratification
  • Working Memory: E.g., following multi-step instructions
  • Flexibility: Adapting to new rules and switching tasks
  • Planning: Breaking tasks into steps and prioritizing actions
  • Self-Monitoring: Evaluating one's own performance and adjusting behavior

Training Strategies

Working Memory

  • Chunking: Break information into smaller parts
  • Visualization: Mental images to remember details
  • Mnemonics: Acronyms or rhymes to remember
  • Brain Games: Chess, Sudoku, or apps

Self-Control

  • 10-Second Rule: Pause before acting and ask yourself "Do I need this?"
  • Remove Temptations: Keep distractions out of sight.
  • Delayed Gratification: Use timers ("I'll check my phone after 1 hour of work")

Flexibility

  • Switch Tasks: Alternate activities (25 minutes of work, 5-minute break)
  • New Routines: E.g. new route to work or use other hand for simple tasks
  • Reframe Problems: Ask "What's another way to solve this?"

Planning

  • TODO: A (urgent), B, C, D, E (unimportant)
  • Time Blocking: Assign specific times for tasks
  • External Tools: Planners, sticky notes, or apps

Self-Monitoring

  • Reflection Questions: End the day with "What worked? What could I improve?"
  • Checklists: Use for routines (e.g., morning/evening habits).
